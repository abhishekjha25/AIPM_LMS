Chapter 01Technical Foundation

AI & ML Foundations

You don't need to write code. But you need to understand how AI works well enough to have credible conversations with ML engineers and spot product risks before they become production incidents.

1.1

Types of Machine Learning

Machine learning teaches computers to make decisions from data rather than explicit rules. As AIPM, knowing which ML type powers which product lets you ask the right questions and set realistic expectations.

🎯
Supervised Learning
Model learns from labelled data — input paired with correct output. Powers spam filters, fraud detection, skip prediction. Most production AI you interact with daily.
🔍
Unsupervised Learning
Model finds patterns without labels. Used for user segmentation, anomaly detection, topic clustering. Netflix finding hidden viewing personas you didn't know existed.
🎮
Reinforcement Learning
Model learns by trial and error, receiving rewards for good actions. Powers Instagram feed ranking and RLHF — the technique that makes ChatGPT feel helpful and aligned.
🧬
Self-Supervised Learning
Model creates its own labels. GPT is pretrained by predicting the next word — the label is always the next token. How all modern LLMs are initially trained on internet data.

Interview Questions
QGoogle · AmazonWhat type of ML powers a recommendation system and why does it matter for a PM?▶
Model Answer

Recommendation systems combine supervised learning (trained on historical interactions as labels) with reinforcement dynamics that update as new interactions arrive.

For the PM, the critical insight: recommendation quality is directly tied to data volume and quality. This is why the cold start problem is a product design challenge, not just an ML one. You solve it through onboarding flows that collect preference signals early.

First question I ask on any recommendation product: What's our data flywheel? If using the product more doesn't improve recommendations, we don't have a moat.

1.2

How Large Language Models Work

LLMs are the engine behind ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. You need to understand the key concepts well enough to set expectations, design fallbacks, and identify where product risk lives.

🔢 Tokenisation
Text split into tokens — roughly words or sub-words. 'Unbelievable' → ['Un','believ','able']. The model never sees letters — only token IDs. Token limits drive cost and context constraints.
🧠 Context Window
How much text the model can process in one request. GPT-4 Turbo: 128K tokens. Claude: 200K tokens. Larger context = more expensive + more latency. As PM, you decide what goes in the context.
🌡️ Temperature
Controls randomness. Temperature 0 = always picks the most probable next token (deterministic). Higher = more creative. Factual Q&A → low. Creative writing → higher. You configure this for your product.
⚠️ Hallucination
The model generates fluent, confident text that is factually wrong. It predicts probable text, not verified facts. This is your single biggest product risk when building with LLMs.
⚠️
Real-world incident: In 2023, two lawyers submitted a brief citing ChatGPT-hallucinated case precedents that didn't exist. The judge fined them $5,000. For legal, medical, or financial products — always implement RAG, source citations, and human review gates.

1.3

Key AI Concepts Every AIPM Must Know

🎯 Precision
Of all positive predictions — how many were actually correct? High precision = few false alarms. Spam filter marking 10 emails where 9 really are spam = 90% precision.
🕵️ Recall
Of all actual positives — how many did the model catch? A spam filter catching 9 out of 10 actual spam emails = 90% recall. Optimise for recall when missing something is costly.
📖 RAG
Retrieval Augmented Generation. Fetch relevant documents at query time and include in the prompt instead of relying on training memory. The standard fix for hallucination in knowledge-intensive products.
📉 Model Drift
Real-world data changes but model was trained on old data. A fraud model from 2022 misses new fraud patterns in 2025. Requires continuous monitoring and periodic retraining.
Precision vs Recall — Choosing the Right Trade-off
Children's Platform Moderation
❌ High precision only = misses subtle harmful content.

✅ High recall = some false positives, but human review catches them. Correct choice — cost of missing harm >> cost of false alarm.
Priority Inbox Feature
❌ High recall = marks everything important, inbox full, feature useless.

✅ High precision = some emails missed, but users trust the feature. Correct choice.

Worked Example — Improving Google Maps (HelloPM)
QGoogleHow would you improve Google Maps? Walk me through your thinking.▶
Full 6-Step Answer (HelloPM Field Guide)

Step 1 — Business Context: Google Maps monetised through ads and API sales. Key goal: increase active users and retention (more screen time = more ad exposure). Trade-off: retention over growth.

Step 2 — User Segments (behaviour-based): Daily commuters, Travelers, Business seekers, Gig workers. Prioritised: Travelers — huge untapped market, many unique problems, more likely to interact with ads.

Step 3 — Problems (latent need): "Accessing all travel-related information from one place" — tickets, identity docs, hotel reservations scattered across apps. Huge anxiety point.

Step 4 — Solutions scored:

  • Manual Upload (Trip Planner): Impact 1, Effort 2, Score 0.5
  • Google Travel Vault (aggregate existing data): Impact 3, Effort 3, Score 1.0
  • Agentic Travel Assistant (proactive, location-aware): Impact 4, Effort 4, Score 1.25 ✓ Winner

Step 5–6 — Metrics + Recommendation: Build an agentic travel planner that aggregates all travel info in one place, bringing users to Google Maps repeatedly during travel → better retention → more data → more ad revenue. Success metric: sessions per traveler trip.

Chapter 03HelloPM Research

User Research

Most startups fail because they build something no one needs. CB Insights: "no market need" is the #1 reason businesses fail at 42%. Research replaces guesses with evidence.

3.1

Why Research Matters

🚫 What Average PMs Do Wrong
Act on opinions (their own, leadership's, or sales') without understanding the basis. Rarely interact with users. Ask leading questions: "Will you find this useful?" who says no?
✅ What Great PMs Do
Validate whether the problem EXISTS AND how important it is. Talk to at least one user every week. Uncover latent needs users can't articulate. Convert insights to roadmap changes immediately.
How Top Companies Ensure Research Happens
Meta: Always asks "What is the people problem behind the product problem?"
Meesho: Takes teams to Tier 2/3 seller offices. New PM hires act as customer support for two weeks.
Intuit "Follow Me Home": PMs visit client offices and observe. Discovered users minimizing QuickBooks and copying data from Excel to paste back — invisible in analytics.
Amazon PRFAQ: Before any budget, you must write a 6-page Press Release + FAQ document from the user's perspective.
3.2

User Interviews & The Mom Test

Humans are biased toward giving positive feedback, not correct feedback. The Mom Test: if you ask the right questions, even your mom cannot lie. Instead of "Mom, am I fat?" — stand on a weighing scale and ask her to read the number. — Rob Fitzpatrick / HelloPM

Bad Questions vs Good Questions
❌ Bad Questions
"Is this a good idea?"
"Would you buy this product?"
"How much would you pay?"
"Would you use an AI PM assistant?"
Ask about the future → get opinions, not data.
✅ Good Questions
"Walk me through your current workflow."
"How are you solving this problem today?"
"Have you tried to find a better solution?"
"Tell me about the last time you tried automating your PM work."
Ask about the past → get facts, not guesses.

People don't buy products — they hire products to do a job in their life. Understanding the real job unlocks better product decisions than demographics or features ever will.

JTBD Statement Format

"When I am [situation], I want to [motivation], so that I can [expected outcome]."

Spotify at gym: "When I'm working out, I want to listen to high-energy music, so that I can stay motivated."

Chapter 04Frameworks & Strategy

Prioritisation & Strategy

Deciding what to build next and how to frame long-term vision — from the Kano Model to Hamilton Helmer's 7 Strategic Powers. The most under-developed skill in product management.

4.1

The Kano Model

Named after Professor Kano. Answers the question every PM faces: "We have 20 ideas and resources for 3. Which 3?" Categorises features based on their relationship to user satisfaction.

🔧
Must-Haves
Users assume these. Absent → strong dissatisfaction. Present → no appreciation.
Examples: Login reliability, UPI in payment apps, calling on a phone
📈
Performance
Satisfaction increases linearly. More = more satisfied. Less = less satisfied.
Examples: Delivery speed, search relevance, battery life, ETA accuracy
✨
Delighters
Disproportionate delight when present. No dissatisfaction when absent.
Examples: AI editing, face unlock, smart recommendations, Gmail attachment reminder

AI-Specific PM Skills

These skills separate an AIPM from a regular PM. Prompt engineering, evals, data strategy, and responsible AI — what interviewers at AI-first companies specifically test for.

5.1

Prompt Engineering & Context Design

Prompt engineering is defining the inputs to an LLM to get the best outputs. As AIPM you define the system prompts that govern how your product's AI behaves for every user. A better prompt can improve your product without any ML work.

System Prompt
Instructions given to the LLM before any user input. Defines persona, constraints, output format, behaviour. This is your core product logic — treat it with the same rigour as code.
Chain of Thought
Add "think step by step before giving your final answer." Improves accuracy on complex reasoning tasks. The model's reasoning becomes visible, making errors easier to catch.
Before & After — Support Ticket Classifier
❌ BEFORE (Naive)
System: "You are a helpful assistant."

// Result: Vague summaries, no structure,
// inconsistent tone, not actionable
✅ AFTER (Engineered)
System: "You are a tier-1 support analyst.
When given a ticket, respond ONLY
with a JSON object:
{
  issue: max 15 words,
  severity: low/medium/high/critical,
  category: billing/technical/account,
  action: one sentence,
  escalate: boolean

5.2

AI Evals — Defining & Measuring Quality

In AI, quality exists on a spectrum. A model might be excellent on 95% of queries and terrible on 5% — and the 5% might be the cases that matter most. AI evals are your structured quality tests. You define what quality means.

📋 Eval Set
A curated collection of input-output pairs defining what 'good' looks like. Minimum 200 examples, ideally 500+. The most important asset you'll build for an AI product.
🤖 LLM-as-Judge
Use a separate LLM to score outputs automatically. Fast, scalable, cheap. Good for catching regressions at scale. Less reliable than human eval for nuanced quality.
👤 Human Eval
Real humans rate model outputs or rank which answer is better. Most accurate signal. Expensive and slow. Use for high-stakes decisions and establishing ground truth.
🔴 Red Teaming
Dedicated sessions where your team deliberately tries to break the model. Do this before every significant launch. Find inputs producing harmful, incorrect, or embarrassing outputs.
💡
Evals are your CI/CD pipeline for AI quality. You wouldn't ship code without tests. Don't ship model changes without evals. Every prompt change or model upgrade triggers a full eval re-run before releasing to production.

5.4

Responsible AI & Ethics

⚠️ Bias in Training Data
Models inherit biases from historical data. A hiring model trained on past hires replicates past hiring biases. A medical model trained mostly on Western patients performs worse on South Asian patients. Audit data sources.
🇪🇺 EU AI Act
Risk-based regulation: Unacceptable (banned) → facial recognition in public. High risk → hiring, medical. Limited risk → chatbots. Know this framework for any product in EU markets.
🇮🇳 India DPDP Act 2023
Data principal consent required. Data minimisation. Right to deletion. Key for any India-facing AI product training on Indian user data.
🔍 Explainability
Can you explain to a user why the AI made a specific decision? For high-stakes decisions (loan applications, medical diagnoses, content moderation) this is both a user need and often a regulatory requirement.

6.1

SQL for Product Managers

SQL is non-negotiable for an AIPM. If you can't query data yourself, you're dependent on analysts with competing priorities. You'll wait days for answers that take 5 minutes. You need 5 SQL patterns that answer 90% of PM questions.

Pattern 1 — COUNT with FILTER: "How many users signed up this month?"
SELECT COUNT(*) AS new_users
FROM users
WHERE country = 'IN'
  AND plan = 'free'
  AND created_at >= '2025-01-01';
Pattern 2 — GROUP BY + ORDER: "Which feature is used most?"
SELECT feature_name, COUNT(*) AS uses
FROM events
GROUP BY feature_name
ORDER BY uses DESC
LIMIT 10;
Pattern 3 — JOIN: "Which users used the AI feature most?"
SELECT u.email, COUNT(e.id) AS ai_queries
FROM users u
JOIN events e ON u.id = e.user_id
WHERE e.event_name = 'ai_query'
GROUP BY u.email
ORDER BY ai_queries DESC;
Pattern 4 — RETENTION RATE: "What % of Jan users came back in Feb?"
SELECT
  COUNT(DISTINCT CASE
    WHEN feb_active = 1 THEN user_id
  END) * 100.0 / COUNT(DISTINCT user_id)
  AS retention_rate
FROM user_cohorts
WHERE cohort_month = '2025-01';
Pattern 5 — AI ACTIVATION: "What % of new users tried AI within 7 days?"
SELECT
  COUNT(DISTINCT CASE
    WHEN ai_first_use_date <= signup_date + 7
    THEN user_id
  END) * 100.0 / COUNT(DISTINCT user_id)
  AS ai_activation_rate
FROM users
WHERE signup_date >= '2025-01-01';

Pattern 4 — Build a Simple RAG System for Your Product Docs
import anthropic

client = anthropic.Anthropic()

# In production, use a vector DB (Pinecone/Weaviate) for retrieval
product_docs = [
    "Feature A does X when user does Y...",
    "Feature B pricing: ₹500/month for Pro...",
    "Known issue: Feature C fails on mobile when..."
]

def answer_from_docs(question: str) -> str:
    context = "\n\n".join(product_docs)
    
    response = client.messages.create(
        model="claude-sonnet-4-5",
        max_tokens=500,
        system=f"""Answer using ONLY this context:
{context}

If the answer isn't in the context, say
"I don't have that info."
Always cite which document you used.""",
        messages=[{"role": "user", "content": question}]
    )
    return response.content[0].text

# Now your team can ask any question about your product
# and get grounded, cited answers instead of hallucinations
answer = answer_from_docs("What does Feature A do?")

QOpenAI · Anthropic 2025Build a quick AI prototype in 30 minutes. Walk us through your thinking. (Vibe coding interview)▶
How to Approach This

This tests product thinking expressed through rapid execution, not coding skill.

First 5 minutes — define what you're building: Don't start building. Say: "I'm going to build [specific feature] for [specific user] because [specific pain]. The core interaction is [X]. I'll use [Replit/v0/Cursor] because [reason]." This is the thinking they're evaluating.

Next 20 minutes — build the MVP:

  • Open Replit or v0.dev. Describe the feature in plain English. Iterate on the output.
  • Focus on the core interaction, not polish. A working system prompt with good structure > a beautiful UI with no AI.
  • Write the system prompt first — this is your product logic, not engineering.

Last 5 minutes — reflect: "What works: [X]. What I'd improve: [Y]. What I'd validate with users: [Z]. The metric I'd track: [A]." Closing with product thinking shows you're a PM who can prototype — not a developer who also does product.

8.2

The 8-Step Answer Framework

Works for product design, improvement, and AI feature questions. Treat as a checklist — by the end of your answer, touch all 8. The most important: name your framework before saying anything else.

1
Name your framework
One sentence before anything else. "I'm prioritising by safety impact and creator growth because those are Instagram's biggest gaps against TikTok." Signals structured thinking immediately.
2
Define the user precisely
Not 'users'. Not 'young people'. "Daily two-wheeler commuters in Indian tier-1 cities navigating dense residential streets." Specificity signals you've actually thought about who you're designing for.
3
State the pain — not the feature
What actually goes wrong in the user's life today? Spend 20-30% of your answer here. Make the interviewer feel the pain before you propose any solution.
4
Propose one P0 feature
One feature only. Resist listing five. Pick one and describe it precisely — what does the user do, what does the product do, what is the exact outcome?
5
Name the real trade-off
Not 'this is hard to build'. The real tension: "We're trading coverage for accuracy" or "We're serving power users at the expense of casual ones." Show you see the cost of your decision.
6
Define a behavioural metric
Not DAU. Not impressions. "% of users who complete the AI-guided loop without skipping." Something that changes only when users are genuinely getting value.
7
Add an adaptive guard rail
What must not get worse — AND what does the system do if it does? "If skip rate exceeds 30%, shift recommendations to highest watch-completion content for that user." Name the recovery path, not just the threshold.
8
Why this company, why now
One sentence: what data, distribution, or model capability does this specific company have that makes this moment right? Shows strategic awareness and competitive thinking.

Full Mock Answers

Product Design
QGoogle Maps IndiaDesign an AI feature for Google Maps for two-wheeler riders in India.▶
Full 8-Step Answer

Framework: Prioritising by safety impact and navigation accuracy — the two biggest gaps for two-wheelers vs car-optimised navigation.

User: Daily two-wheeler commuters in Indian tier-1 and tier-2 cities navigating dense traffic, narrow lanes, and road conditions not reflected in standard map data.

Problem: Google Maps is designed for cars. Two-wheelers can use lanes too narrow to appear on maps, navigate through traffic gaps, and are far more vulnerable to potholes. Current product gives wrong ETAs (too slow), wrong routes (too wide), and zero hazard information.

Feature P0: Two-Wheeler Mode — dedicated navigation using crowdsourced two-wheeler journey data to suggest faster routes including narrow lanes, with real-time road condition warnings (potholes, water-logging) contributed by other riders.

Trade-off: Crowdsourced data quality is variable — early coverage sparse outside metros. Launch in top 5 cities where data density is highest. Accept lower coverage in exchange for higher accuracy where data exists.

Metric: Two-wheeler ETA accuracy (predicted vs actual journey time) and route adherence rate.

Guard rail: Don't show hazard warnings with fewer than 5 independent reports — a false warning erodes trust faster than no warning. Recovery: auto-hide warnings that haven't been confirmed in 24h.

Why Google now: 50M+ monthly active Maps users in India. Waze crowdsource infrastructure already exists. No competitor has this data density at scale.

Safety & Ethics (Anthropic/OpenAI specific)
QAnthropic · OpenAIHow would you handle a safety concern that conflicts with a product launch deadline?▶
Model Answer

My answer is direct: the safety concern takes precedence. Always. The product launch deadline moves.

Why: A safety incident at an AI company is not just a product problem. It's a mission-alignment failure, a regulatory risk, and a trust crisis that takes years to recover from. Delaying a launch by 2 weeks is recoverable. A safety incident involving real user harm is not.

How I'd handle the pressure:

  • Be specific about the concern: what exactly is the safety risk, its probability, and potential severity? Vague concerns don't stop launches. Specific, documented, high-severity risks do.
  • Propose a mitigation path: can we scope the launch to exclude the risky use case? Add a human review gate? Launch to a limited audience first?
  • Escalate if needed: if the team is under pressure to ignore a credible safety risk, I escalate. This is what safety teams exist for.

What I'd never do: "Ship and monitor." That's a plan to harm users while generating evidence to fix the problem. Never acceptable for a credible safety concern.

Specifically asked at Anthropic and OpenAI PM interviews. Your answer signals mission alignment.
Estimation
QGoogle · McKinseyEstimate how many AI assistant queries are sent globally per day in 2025.▶
Model Answer

Building from the bottom up using published data as anchors:

  • ChatGPT: ~200M weekly actives (2025). Average 5 queries/user/day = ~1 billion queries/day
  • Gemini (Google): 8.5B searches/day. 10% involve AI = ~850M AI queries/day
  • Microsoft Copilot: 400M 365 subscribers. 5% daily active at 3 queries = ~60M/day
  • Claude (Anthropic): Smaller base. ~50M/day
  • Others (Meta AI, Perplexity, Chinese platforms): ~600M/day combined

Total estimate: ~2.5 billion AI assistant queries per day globally.

Sanity check: 4 billion internet users. 2.5B queries implies ~1 in 1.6 internet users sends an AI query daily — plausible given AI is now embedded in search, email, and coding tools. Range: 1.5 to 4 billion/day.

8.4

Common Mistakes to Avoid

🚫 Jumping to features
The #1 mistake. Spend 30-40% of your answer establishing the user and problem BEFORE naming a single feature. Interviewers watch specifically for this discipline. You can be rejected in the first 2 minutes.
👤 Vague user definitions
'Young users' and 'enterprise customers' are demographics, not users. Define by behaviour and context: "First-time managers at startups under 200 people who've never had management training."
✂️ Not killing alternatives
In prioritisation questions, explicitly explain why each rejected option lost. Weak candidates defend their picks. Strong candidates also kill the alternatives with specific reasoning.
📊 Vanity metrics
If your success metric could go up even when the product isn't helping users, it's vanity. 'Impressions', 'clicks' without context. Go one layer deeper every time.
🤖 Ignoring AI failure modes
For any AI feature question, always address: what happens when the AI is wrong? What's the fallback? Ignoring this makes you look like a PM who doesn't understand probabilistic systems.
🛡️ No guard rail
Every metric needs a guard rail AND a recovery path. Every interviewer knows that optimising engagement without a guard rail gave us infinite scroll and social media addiction.
